Chapter 5: What else is happening?

Court cases

The Supreme Court of Canada—especially since the 1970s—has been strengthening Indigenous rights and title as set out in the Constitution of Canada and its supporting legislation. The Court has ruled in favour of many Indigenous groups. Here are a few interesting court cases that are defining the way forward.

The Delgamuukw case

The Delgamuukw case (1997) argued the definition of Aboriginal title. The Supreme Court of Canada maintained that Aboriginal title was an ancestral right protected by Section 35(1) of the Constitution Act, 1982. Read about this case here.

The Sparrow Case

Ronald Edward Sparrow was convicted but then later cleared by the Supreme Court for fishing illegally. However, his ancestral right to fishing was upheld. Read about this case here.

The Marshall case

A member of the Membertou First Nation was accused of fishing out of season without a license, and selling his catch without a license. In September 1999, the Supreme Court of Canada confirmed that Donald Marshall Jr. had a treaty right to catch and sell fish. Read about this case here.

The Tsilhqot’in Case

During the Chilcotin War of 1864, Tsilhqot’in chiefs were invited to peacekeeping talks. Instead, they were arrested, tried and hanged for the deaths of members of a non-Indigenous road crew. In 2018, Prime Minister Justin Trudeau made an official apology for the wrongful hanging. Read about this case here.
