Modern treaties

The Government of Canada has frequently been found guilty of breaching treaty obligations by the courts. The court cases were the first step in a long process that has led to today’s modern treaties, also known as comprehensive claims agreements.

Land claims generally arise in areas of Canada where Indigenous land rights have not been dealt with by treaty or other legal means. In these areas, forward-looking comprehensive claims agreements are negotiated between Indigenous groups and the federal, provincial and territorial governments. They are implemented through legislation and they remain the most comprehensive way of addressing Indigenous rights and title. Some of these treaties have also included provisions relating to self-government.

Since 1973, the Government of Canada and its negotiation partners have signed 26 comprehensive land claims and 4 self-government agreements. Today, modern treaties cover over 40% of Canada’s land mass. They include Government of Canada obligations such as hiring a certain number of people from the treaty area, or contracting to local companies. Some have even involved foreign trade.

Do you know of any treaty obligations in your area of work? Chances are there are some.

The rights set out in historical and modern treaties are protected under the Constitution of Canada. They are as applicable today as when they were first signed.

As public service employees, we need to:

  • be aware of our roles and responsibilities
  • work collaboratively with colleagues in Crown-Indigenous Relations and Northern Affairs Canada (previously known as Indigenous and Northern Affairs Canada)
