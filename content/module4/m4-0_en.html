Chapter 4: Talking about the land

First Nations, Métis and Inuit share a profound connection to the land. Chapter 4 explores this sacred relationship and how it impacts treaties and agreements with Indigenous peoples.

The land is not owned, nor does it belong to any one person.

A sacred relationship

Connection with the land is a sacred relationship for Indigenous peoples. Like a mother provides for her children, the earth gives us food, water and shelter—everything we need to survive and more. In return, we have a responsibility to take care of Mother Earth.

From this perspective, the land is not an object. The land is a living entity that guides our health and well-being—mentally, physically, emotionally and spiritually.

When Mother Earth is sick, we are sick.

Environmentalists advocate that caring for the earth is not just the responsibility of Indigenous peoples. It is the responsibility of all humans around the world. The earth can survive without us, but we cannot survive without the earth. If we don’t take care of our environment, future generations will suffer.

The land can also mean different things to different Indigenous communities and people. For example, a person who is Plains Cree will have a different understanding of the land than an Inuk, based on differing world views and landscapes.

As public service employees, we need to respect this sacred connection to the land—even if we do not understand it.
