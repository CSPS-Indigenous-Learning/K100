Duty to consult and accommodate

Because of section 35 of the Constitution Act, 1982, public service employees have a duty to consult and accommodate regarding any activity that could interfere with Indigenous right.

Reactive versus proactive

Prior to the Supreme Court case Haida Nation v. British Columbia (Minister of Forests) in 2004, it was considered ‘’acceptable’’ to take a reactive approach to Indigenous rights and title claims. Once a project was in place, the Government of Canada would review any complaints it received and react as fit. This period is known as pre-Haida.

Since the 2004 Supreme Court case, or post-Haida, a reactive approach is no longer acceptable. The Government of Canada must be proactive. This means that as employees of the Government of Canada we must:

  • consult with Indigenous communities whenever Indigenous rights or title could be impacted
  • be proactive in working with First Nations, Métis and Inuit before planning, developing, implementing and monitoring anything that could affect their rights
  • go out and listen to what First Nations, Métis and Inuit communities want and need
  • work collaboratively with Indigenous peoples in developing new policies, programs and services

The courts will not be in a position to support government initiatives if consultation has not taken place in good faith.

As a public service employee, much of what you do has a direct or indirect impact on the rights of First Nations, Métis or Inuit—especially in developing legislation, policies, operational procedures, or services.

Self-reflection: In the work you do, do you have an obligation to consult with Indigenous peoples? If not, how could it be beneficial to consult with Indigenous communities?
