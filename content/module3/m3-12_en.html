Examples of inequities for Indigenous Peoples

Through your work, personal life or the media, you may be aware of differences in health and social status between Indigenous people and non-Indigenous people in our country. These differences have increased over time and have a significant impact on the well-being of First Nations, Métis and Inuit.

The following table highlights some of the differences between the lived realities of Indigenous and non-Indigenous people. It uses Statistics Canada data from the Census in 2011 and 2016.

Poverty

Statistics Canada considers low income to be $22,133 for one person.

  • 81% of Indigenous communities on reserves had a median income below low income
  • 27 communities reported median total incomes below $10,000
  • Average income for Indigenous people is $25,526, compared with $34,604 for the rest of the country
Housing and Living Conditions

In general, the proportion of people living in a home that needed major repairs decreased for First Nations people, Métis and Inuit communities from 2011 to 2016. The only exception was on reserve, where the proportion of registered and status First Nations people living in homes that needed major repairs went up by 0.8%.

In addition, one-quarter of Inuit households in Inuit Nunangat were crowded in 2016. This group also had the largest increase since 2006.

Two fifths (40.6%) of the Inuit population lived in crowded housing in 2016

One quarter (23.1%) of First Nations people lived in crowded housing in 2016

About one in ten (8.6%) Métis lived in housing that was crowded

Suicide Crisis

Suicidal thoughts may be under-reported owing to the stigma attached to suicide.

Nearly one quarter of First Nations people living off-reserve (24.0%) and Inuit (23.5%) and one in five Métis (19.6%) reported having had suicidal thoughts

First Nations, Métis and Inuit women were more likely than non-Indigenous women to report suicidal thoughts (13.8%)

Crime Rates

12.68 Indigenous people per 100,000 Indigenous people were the victims of homicide in the Northwest Territories

8,262 Indigenous youth were admitted to territorial and provincial correctional services throughout Canada

23.8% of Indigenous offenders eligible for full parole were granted release in Canada, whereas 35.5% of non-Indigenous offenders were eligible and granted release

Indigenous people make up 22% of those sentenced to custody in the provincial or federal correctional systems

Child Welfare

About 4,300 Indigenous children under age 4 living in foster care make up more than half (52.2%) of all foster children, although they only make up 7.7% of the total child population
